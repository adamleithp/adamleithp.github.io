I'm writing this to inform others like me, who want an idea into how easy it is to travel South East Asia with nothing in your bag.

South East Asia is the perfect place to break into backpacking/traveling.

Before I start, I'm going to emphasize one thing: You can buy almost everything you need once in Asia- for cheaper. Things like, ~real~ products, Nutella, peanut butter and poutine will be expensive as it is at home. :( Poutine...

Documents

  • Passport (duhh)

  • Passport photos (you can get these done for cheaper once in Asia, but having them beforehand will help you out.)

  • Vaccination booklet. Although no one ever asked to see this booklet, bring it with you.

Equipment / Stuff

~Big~ Backpack

30L - 40L backpack with internal frame.

The only times you're actually wearing these ~big~ bags is when you get off a bus/van and are looking for accommodations and vice-versa; The most I spent wearing this ~big~ bag was at most 15 minutes at a time.

Almost everyone I encountered who was backpacking through SEA (South East Asia) was carrying 55L - 70L bags; Everyone travels differently, I just think these bags as far too large for this kind of climate.

I used a Osprey Farpoint 40L. It was perfect for carrying the essentials (listed here) and some gifts. Osprey Farpoint 40L is a great versatile bag which is carry-on compliant with all airlines.

Carry-on vs not.

Keep all your stuff with you when travelling: Some people like to dump their bags at check-in and forget them, very understandable- but I don't like to do this. My view is that when you check your bag, the chances of your belongings being lost during transtiion potentially jumped from 0% to 100%. Nope, no thanks.

No wait times for baggage carousels: When you arrive at your destination, you don't have to wait around the metal conveyer belt like the rest of the chumps- no, you get to walk right past these death traps and go right to immigration, no line, no hassle- Beautiful.

Unleash the hippy...

I met people only using only a 25L backpacks. These people weren’t dirty, their clothes maybe were, but they're doing it right. After your first few months in SEA, you will eventually become this person: wearing the same clothes each day, not caring if you smell etc. But until then, make the natural transition into bliss like the rest of us. :)

Small Backpack

A very comfortable daypack; 5L - 15L

The most important thing you will carry.

This backpack is what you will be using 99% of the time. It's your life, it's what leaves the room with you when you go and adventure. I liked a bigger 15L bag as I like to keep: a bigger medical kit on me at all times (Saved me in Myanmar), Go Pro camera with all it's accessories, and a few books.

Medical Kit(s)

I have two of these, because when your big bag is stuck under the bus, my day pack comes to the rescue.

DayPack Medical Kit

This stuff is on you at all times,

  • Full bottle of Tylenol.

  • An assortment of Bandades

  • Salene solution or rubbing alcohol

  • Diarrhea pills (never used)

  • Anti-Acid tablets. (I never needed these before Asia)

~Big~ Medical Kit

  • Advil (never used)

  • Full package of Bandades, all sizes.

  • Antihistamines (BENADRYL or whatever) for allergic reactions to anything. (never used)

  • Diarrhea pills (never used)

Headlamp

One of the most useful things you can bring.

Why do you need this? Walking in the dark and navigating a dark dorm room for your stuff quickly comes to mind. Just keep it in your day pack and you'll feel like a super badass when it comes time to use it.

Electronics

Gopro Hero 3+

Self explanatory, take pictures/video, connect the iPad to its wifi, download and upload the photos/video to the web.

iPad (jailbroken)

Self explanatory. Why I specified the iPad was jailbroken (hacked if you will) is that I downloaded something called "safari downloader" which allowed me to download items off the internet.

Kindle

Self explanatory, reading feeds the soul.

I would get eBooks from my iPad which I would then send to my amazon email, which would then be downloaded automatically to the Kindle.

Clothing

Good Footwear (1-2x)

Most people have a pair of throw-away flip flops that they use 99% of the time. Personally, I cannot use these as they have no foot support.

I used my trusty Birkenstocks, wore them 95% of my entire trip. Comfortable, healthy arch support, high quality fabrication, and Germans will love you for wearing them outside your Haus (house)!

I also brought a pair of Columbia waterproof trekking shoes. I've tried trekking in Birkenstocks, and it's not so fun.

Travel towel (1x)

Your towel is about the most massively useful thing an interstellar hitchhiker can have – I mean backpacker. Any camping towel or a fancy travel towel will do. They are there to dry your wet ass.

Pants (0-1x)

Not necessary at all if in warm climates. You will be buying those super baggy pants anyway, because they are just the most comfortable thing you can wear.

I personally brought my trusty climbing pants that could roll up the lefts. These were a mighty useful in northern half of Vietnam (in December the temperature hovers around ~15ºC)

Shorts (2x)

  • 1 pair of high-tech trekking shorts which that will double as swimsuit. I used a pair of Nike Dry Fits pants.

  • 1 normal looking Adidas shorts.

Shirts (1-3x)

I cannot stress this enough, you can buy shirts in Asia for 1 / 5 the price -or less- you would pay back home.

Socks (0-2x)

This is a luxury if it’s cold in the bus, van or airplane. Basically, if you’re like me- from Canada- you’ll be absolutely fine without socks.
